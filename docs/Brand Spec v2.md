# R1 Brand Spec v2

_Last updated: 2026-04-12_

---

## What changed from v1

The v1 Brand Spec defined a strong visual direction but left several critical questions unanswered: Robin's role in the brand identity, tone of voice, how to present the consultation step as premium rather than clinical, and how the brand story evolves when the estrogen/Rx layer activates. This draft addresses those gaps.

---

## 1. Brand identity

### Name

"R1" is a working placeholder. Final naming work is still needed. Whatever the name, it should:
- feel like a destination, not a product
- be short, distinctive, ownable
- signal precision and sophistication without coldness
- work across beauty and (eventually) clinical contexts without whiplash

Naming direction: think words associated with regeneration, protocol, origin, renewal — not words associated with "youth," "anti-aging," or generic wellness.

### Robin's role in the brand

R1 is **Robin Berzin's protocol made accessible** — not a generic skincare line that happens to have an MD behind it. The brand carries her credibility and her specific functional medicine + skin science perspective. But R1 stands on its own as a brand with its own visual identity, voice, and world.

The right framing: she is the **founder and scientific authority**, not the spokesperson. Her name and face appear as trust signal, not as the product itself. Think of it the way Augustinus Bader works — the scientist is visible, but the brand transcends the individual.

In practice:
- Homepage: "Developed by Dr. Robin Berzin, MD" — present but not dominant
- Quiz results: signed by Robin's voice, warm and expert
- Science pages: Dr. Berzin's editorial perspective front and center
- Product copy: protocol-led, not founder-testimonial
- Photography: occasional doctor/founder presence, but not every surface

Her existing Parsley Health and RobinBerzinMD.com audience is a launch asset. The brand should be clearly connected to her ecosystem while feeling like its own premium destination — not a Parsley extension.

## 2. Brand positioning

### The category R1 plays in

**Premium clinical beauty with a regenerative science framework.**

Not wellness. Not telehealth. Not luxury-for-luxury's-sake. Not a supplement. R1 is where high-performance skincare meets functional medicine — for women who have already tried the "good" products and want something that works from the inside out and the outside in with real science behind it.

### The positioning statement

> *The first skincare experience built around your biology. Doctor-developed, personally guided, designed to regenerate — not just maintain.*

### The hero promise (choose one for hero copy)

Options in order of preference:
1. **"Regenerative skincare that works with your biology."** — most differentiated, most Robin
2. **"Your skin's biology doesn't slow down at 35. Your skincare shouldn't either."** — emotional hook, very targetted
3. **"Doctor-developed. Personally guided. Actually works."** — clear, results-forward

Recommendation: option 1 as hero headline, option 3 as supporting subhead. Option 2 for email/social creative.

### What R1 is not

- Not wellness branding (no crystals, affirmations, or "glow" without substance)
- Not telehealth UX (no clinical intake forms, no cold medical design language)
- Not supplement brand design (no green-dominant palettes or capsule photography)
- Not crunchy/natural skincare (no earthy textures, craft aesthetics, "clean" claims without performance)
- Not mass premium beauty (no Sephora aesthetic, no splashy before/afters)
- Not biohacker (not OneSkin, not cold/algorithmic, not male-coded)
- Not anti-aging by erasure — R1 is about regeneration and vitality, not fighting time

## 3. Target customer

### Primary audience

Women 35–60, concentrated in the 38–52 range (peri- and early post-menopause).

**Who she is:**
- Health-informed and intellectually curious about her body
- Has likely tried premium skincare (La Mer, SkinCeuticals, Tatcha) and found it insufficient or confusing
- Trusts Robin Berzin or the functional medicine worldview
- Cares about visible results AND understanding why things work
- Not looking for the cheapest version — looking for the most effective version
- Comfortable spending $150–400+ on a skincare regimen she believes in

**What she's frustrated by:**
- Generic skincare that doesn't account for hormonal shifts
- Too many products with no coherent system
- Confusing retinol advice (strong prescription retinoids vs. useless OTC concentrations)
- Feeling like there's no real medical intelligence behind most skincare

**What she wants to feel:**
- "I finally have a protocol that's actually designed for me."
- "Someone who really understands skin has looked at my specific situation."
- "I trust this to actually work."

### Secondary audience

Women 35+ already in the Parsley/Robin Berzin orbit — existing patients, newsletter subscribers, podcast listeners. This group has high trust and is easiest to convert early.

## 4. Brand voice and tone

### Voice in one sentence

*Clinical intelligence delivered with warmth. Like a doctor who's also your most trusted friend.*

### Tone principles

**Expert, not preachy.** R1 speaks with authority but never lectures. It teaches and informs because knowledge builds trust, not because the customer needs to be educated.

**Warm, not soft.** This brand is feminine without being gentle. The warmth is in the relationship, not the words. Precision and care coexist.

**Specific, not vague.** "Vitamin A esters that convert to retinoids in the skin" is better than "our advanced formula." This audience respects specificity.

**Confident, not arrogant.** R1 doesn't oversell. It makes clear, backed claims and lets the product work. No breathless beauty-brand enthusiasm.

**Robin's voice when it matters.** In consultation result delivery, in science content, in protocol explanation — Robin's specific perspective, vocabulary, and warmth should come through. Not corporate brand voice.

### Vocabulary to use

- regenerate / regeneration
- protocol
- biology / your biology
- skin longevity
- cellular
- your skin's natural capacity
- from the inside out / from the outside in
- step-up system
- guided
- precision skincare
- your protocol

### Vocabulary to avoid

- anti-aging (use: skin longevity, regenerative)
- glowing (overused, vague)
- radiant (same)
- revolutionary (use: clinical, precise, proven)
- natural (use: biology-led, science-backed)
- clean (not the right framing for this brand)
- transformation (overpromised)
- luxury (let it be felt, not stated)

## 5. Visual strategy

### Core principle

*Results + premium feel + guided action.*

The visual world should communicate: "Something serious and beautiful is happening here." Every surface should feel like it's worth the investment before the customer has even read a word.

### Above the fold hierarchy

1. Efficacy / results promise (headline)
2. Premium visual world (imagery)
3. Quiz / consultation CTA (primary action)
4. Robin's authority (subtle, supporting)

### Color palette

**Palette philosophy:** warmth over stark contrast, restraint over decoration, femininity through tone not saturation, depth over brightness.

**Core palette:**

| Role | Direction |
|------|-----------|
| Base | warm ivory, soft cream, porcelain beige |
| Neutrals | mushroom, taupe, warm stone, putty |
| Deep anchor | espresso, plum-charcoal, soft chocolate — one of these as the primary dark |
| Accent | muted bronze, brushed brass, dusty mauve, soft oxblood — used sparingly |

**Avoid:** stark black/white biohacker contrast, green-heavy natural palettes, bright coral, telehealth blue, soft pink wellness palettes.

### Typography

**Two-font system:**

| Role | Direction |
|------|-----------|
| Serif (headlines + brand expression) | Modern, editorial, intelligent, premium. Not ornate. Not fashion-only. Should carry beauty and sophistication. |
| Sans serif (body + UI) | Clean, sharp, precise. Not startup-generic. Not rounded. Should carry clarity and clinical trust. |

Direction: Serif = beauty and emotion. Sans = performance and intelligence. Together: premium performance beauty.

Candidate serif references: Cormorant Garamond, Portrait, Canela, Graphik Condensed (used editorially), Freight Display.
Candidate sans references: Neue Haas Grotesk, Söhne, Inter (only if not startup-coded in context), GT Walsheim.

### Photography direction

**Show:**
- Luminous, healthy skin on real adult women (35–55)
- Confidence and composure — not performance of youth
- Premium product presence: close-up texture, pump, glass packaging
- Clean interiors and surfaces — not clinical, not bohemian
- Light that feels warm, intentional, and expensive

**Avoid:**
- Lab or clinical environments
- Before-and-after photography (too mass-market / Sephora)
- Soft bohemian wellness (plants, ceramics, linens)
- Gen Z beauty aesthetics (glossy, playful, saturated)
- Founder-dominant imagery on every surface

### UI / layout principles

- Clean, disciplined layouts with strong hierarchy
- Product-forward structure — let the product imagery breathe
- Elegant spacing and restraint
- Minimal but intentional color
- Subtle integration of science / proof (ingredient callouts, protocol stages, efficacy notes in supporting text)
- Quiz UI should feel like a premium concierge experience, not a form

## 6. Visual references

### Primary references (design against)

| Brand | What to borrow | What to avoid |
|-------|---------------|---------------|
| **Augustinus Bader** | Prestige, product-led luxury, restrained, credible, expensive | Price-tier association ($400+ creams) — R1 should feel accessible within premium range |
| **Merit Beauty** | Modern feminine aesthetic, refined simplicity, warmth without softness | The youth skew in some campaigns |
| **Timeline (longevity supplement brand)** | Clean premium science, longevity cues, sharp intelligent design | Coldness, the slight biohacker feel |

### Secondary references

| Brand | What to borrow |
|-------|---------------|
| **Alloy** | Trustworthy clinical clarity for midlife women, age-specific relevance; avoid their telehealth UX patterns |
| **Barbara Sturm** | Authority and medical-grade positioning; avoid coldness and sterility |

### Target synthesis

Merit's femininity + Augustinus Bader's prestige + Timeline's scientific sophistication.

More feminine and beautiful than Timeline. Less clinical than Barbara Sturm. Less telehealth than Alloy. Less crunchy than Tata Harper. Less biohacker than OneSkin.

|-----------|----------------|

| Luxury vs. utility | Premium, justified by performance — not luxury for its own sake |

## 8. The consultation as brand expression

This is new to v2, and important.

The human consultation step is not just a compliance requirement — it is a brand moment. In a market full of algorithm-driven quiz results, the fact that a real trained specialist reviews your skin before making a recommendation is a premium differentiator that should be named and celebrated.

**How to frame the consultation in brand copy:**

Not: "Your submission is being reviewed."
Instead: "Your personalized skin consultation is being prepared by [Consultant Name], a trained skin specialist."

Not: "Please wait 24 hours."
Instead: "In the next 24 hours, [Name] will review your skin profile and design your personal protocol."

Not: "Consult required before purchase."
Instead: "Every R1 protocol begins with a real consultation — because your skin is specific to you."

The 24-hour wait between quiz and protocol delivery is a premium experience cue. Use it. Fill it with education about the ingredients, the science, and what to expect — so the customer arrives at the protocol reveal feeling informed and excited, not just waiting.

## 9. Estrogen / clinical lane — brand implications

The estrogen/Rx lane is not live in v1, but its eventual activation should already be planted in the brand.

The seed language:
> *"For women 40+, R1's clinical extension adds a prescription topical estrogen option, developed to complement your protocol — when you're ready."*

Position it on the site as an exclusive next chapter, not a medical detour. The transition from "premium skincare" to "personalized clinical skincare" should feel like R1 getting more powerful for you, not like switching brands.

Design recommendation: reserve a `future-estrogen` page from day 1 — waitlist capture with brand-appropriate copy. No clinical intake language at this stage.

## 11. DALL-E prompts for key brand visuals

Use these as starting points to generate reference imagery for the design process.

**Hero/homepage image:**
> Photorealistic editorial beauty photograph. A woman, late 40s, luminous and composed. Warm ivory background. Close-up on skin and profile. Natural light from the left with a warm golden cast. Skin looks healthy, firm, radiant — not airbrushed into youth, but clearly vital. Premium skincare brand aesthetic. Editorial, restrained, expensive. Inspired by Augustinus Bader campaigns. No text. 16:9 crop.

**Product photography:**
> Still life product photography. A minimalist glass pump bottle with warm ivory and brushed brass details. Soft cream or porcelain beige surface. Clean, warm studio light with soft shadows. Premium skincare brand. Restrained, expensive, clinical-adjacent but beautiful. Influenced by Augustinus Bader and Merit Beauty product imagery. No text. Square crop.

**Quiz/consultation illustration:**
> Clean editorial illustration or abstract art. Flowing organic forms suggesting skin texture at a cellular level — soft, warm ivory and taupe tones with a muted bronze accent. Minimal. Modern. Suggests biology and regeneration without being clinical or scientific-diagram-like. Suitable as a quiz background or section divider. No text.

**Protocol reveal:**
> Photorealistic close-up of a woman's hands holding a printed card or looking at a phone screen showing a personalized skincare protocol. Warm, soft ambient light. Premium feel — cream-colored materials, elegant typography. A sense of anticipation and care. No literal product visible.

**Inside-out / lifestyle:**
> Editorial lifestyle photograph. A woman in her 40s, morning light. She's at a clean kitchen counter with a glass of water and a small supplement. Calm, composed, intelligent energy. Not staged wellness. Warm but sharp. Like the first 10 minutes of a very organized morning. Premium, adult, intentional.
